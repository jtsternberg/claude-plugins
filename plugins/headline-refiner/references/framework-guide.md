# Reference Documentation for Headline Refiner

## The 5-Part Headline Framework

Every effective headline answers four critical reader questions: "How long will this take? What am I getting? Is this for me? Why should I care?"

### 1. The Number

Before a reader gives attention, they perform a quick cost-benefit analysis: *perceived value ÷ perceived time investment*.

The number signals time investment. Compare:
- "3 steps to brainstorm, build, and sell your first digital product" ✓
- "300 steps to brainstorm, build, and sell your first digital product" ✗

Listicles have a bad reputation (BuzzFeed association), but they work because:
- Easy for writers to organize ideas
- Easy for readers to evaluate time commitment
- Clear scope and expectation setting

**Questions to answer:**
- How many things are being promised?
- How can time investment be signaled immediately?

### 2. The What

The biggest mistake: being clever instead of clear.

**Examples of what NOT to do:**
- "Whispers In The Wind"
- "A Lesson for the Ages"
- "Disrupting the World One Pixel at a Time"

The reality: if readers don't understand what the piece is about, they won't try to figure it out. They'll scroll past.

**Rule of thumb:** If confused, the reader is gone.

Make it painfully clear what you're giving the reader:
- Habits?
- Templates?
- Personality traits?
- Strategies?
- Lessons?

**Example of "what" specificity:**
- "The five pieces of an effective headline" (pieces/components)
- "The five emotional qualities of an effective headline" (emotional elements)
- "The five psychological hooks of an effective headline" (psychological techniques)

Same topic, but the "what" changes the content completely.

### 3. The Who

The target audience can be **explicit** or **implied**.

**Implied example:**
"The five pieces of an effective headline" → Anyone who wants to learn headline writing

**Explicit example:**
"The five pieces of an effective headline every political speech writer should know" → Political speech writers specifically

**Pro tip for newer writers:** Name the target audience directly in the headline for two reasons:

1. **Resonance**: Anyone who identifies with that title will see themselves in the headline
2. **Distribution**: Social platforms and search engines crawl every word. Naming the audience helps algorithms distribute to the right people, even with zero followers.

### 4. The Why

The most underrated part of any headline. The "why" is the mini sales pitch.

To get readers to give full attention, don't just tell them what the piece is about—tell them why it's in their best interest to read.

**Two approaches:**

**Positive outcome (read this to unlock...):**
"The five pieces of an effective headline that guarantee readers will give you their full attention"

**Negative outcome (read this to avoid...):**
"The five pieces of an effective headline—without these, you'll never become a successful writer"

### 5. Twisting the Knife

A copywriting technique: give the reader more of what they want.

**For positive outcomes, stack benefits:**
"The five pieces of an effective headline that guarantee readers will give you their full attention, fall in love with your writing, and support your work forever"

**For negative outcomes, make them more painful and urgent:**
"The five pieces of an effective headline—without these, you'll never become proficient, prolific, or successful as a writer"

The more outcomes listed, the more the target reader is pulled in. It demonstrates understanding of what they care about.

## Common Objection: "My headline will be too long!"

This is wrong. People read long headlines all the time—as long as the headline is **clear and relevant**.

The reader doesn't care if it's 3 words or 13 words. They don't want wasted time, which has nothing to do with word count and everything to do with **clarity of message and value**.

**Platform constraints exist** (YouTube Shorts: ~6 words, email subjects: ~9 words), but the process is:
1. Go through all five pieces first
2. Get clarity over what you're giving, who it's for, why they should read
3. THEN whittle down to fit platform constraints

Don't start with the constraint and try to be quippy.

## Analysis Checklist

When refining a headline, verify each component:

| Component | Question | Check |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| Number | How many things are promised? | □ |
| What | Is it painfully clear what the reader gets? | □ |
| Who | Is the audience explicit or appropriately implied? | □ |
| Why | Is there a positive OR negative outcome promised? | □ |
| Twist | Can the knife be twisted with additional outcomes? | □ |

**Weak:** "Productivity Advice"
**Strong:** "3 morning routines that doubled my output—without waking up earlier"

**Weak:** "Marketing Strategies"
